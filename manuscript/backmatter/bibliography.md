# Bibliography

---

## Social Choice Theory and Voting

Arrow, K. J. (1951). *Social Choice and Individual Values*. John Wiley & Sons. [The foundational impossibility theorem. Chapter 7 of this book provides the geometric reinterpretation.]

Arrow, K. J. (1963). *Social Choice and Individual Values* (2nd ed.). Yale University Press. [Expanded edition with the Notes on the Theory of Social Choice.]

Black, D. (1948). On the rationale of group decision-making. *Journal of Political Economy*, 56(1), 23-34. [The median voter theorem — the 1D special case where Arrow's impossibility does not apply.]

Condorcet, Marquis de (1785). *Essai sur l'application de l'analyse a la probabilite des decisions rendues a la pluralite des voix*. [The original statement of the Condorcet paradox: cyclic majority preferences.]

Gibbard, A. (1973). Manipulation of voting schemes: A general result. *Econometrica*, 41(4), 587-601. [Strategic voting is unavoidable in non-dictatorial systems with 3+ alternatives.]

McKelvey, R. D. (1976). Intransitivities in multidimensional voting models and some implications for agenda control. *Journal of Economic Theory*, 12(3), 472-482. [The chaos theorem: in multi-dimensional voting, majority rule can cycle through the entire policy space.]

Satterthwaite, M. A. (1975). Strategy-proofness and Arrow's conditions. *Journal of Economic Theory*, 10(2), 187-217. [Independent discovery of the Gibbard result.]

Sen, A. K. (1970). *Collective Choice and Social Welfare*. Holden-Day. [Extended social choice theory to include capabilities, interpersonal comparisons, and multi-dimensional welfare.]

Sen, A. K. (1999). *Development as Freedom*. Knopf. [The capability approach as manifold coordinates — each capability a dimension of human welfare.]

## Spatial Voting and Political Science

Downs, A. (1957). *An Economic Theory of Democracy*. Harper & Row. [The median voter theorem applied to party competition. The 1D model that this book generalizes to 6D.]

Poole, K. T., & Rosenthal, H. (1985). A spatial model for legislative roll call analysis. *American Journal of Political Science*, 29(2), 357-384. [The DW-NOMINATE model. Chapter 1 of this book critiques the inference from 1D voting data to 1D preferences.]

Poole, K. T., & Rosenthal, H. (2007). *Ideology and Congress* (2nd ed.). Transaction Publishers. [Full development of the DW-NOMINATE methodology and its application to 200 years of congressional voting.]

Enelow, J. M., & Hinich, M. J. (1984). *The Spatial Theory of Voting: An Introduction*. Cambridge University Press. [Multi-dimensional spatial voting theory — the mathematical precursor to the manifold approach.]

## Polarization and Sorting

Fiorina, M. P., Abrams, S. J., & Pope, J. C. (2005). *Culture War? The Myth of a Polarized America*. Pearson Longman. [Early argument that policy polarization is exaggerated; sorting is the primary phenomenon.]

Mason, L. (2018). *Uncivil Agreement: How Politics Became Our Identity*. University of Chicago Press. [Sorting, not policy polarization, drives affective polarization — a thesis this book formalizes geometrically.]

Iyengar, S., & Westwood, S. J. (2015). Fear and loathing across party lines: New evidence on group polarization. *American Journal of Political Science*, 59(3), 690-707. [Empirical measurement of affective polarization as partisan animus.]

## Redistricting and Gerrymandering

McGhee, E. (2014). Measuring partisan bias in single-member district electoral systems. *Legislative Studies Quarterly*, 39(1), 55-85. [The efficiency gap as a gerrymandering metric. Chapter 9 of this book extends this to the manifold.]

Duchin, M. (2018). Gerrymandering metrics: How to measure? What's the baseline? *Notices of the American Mathematical Society*. [The ensemble approach to redistricting analysis.]

Duchin, M., & Tenner, B. E. (2018). Discrete geometry for electoral geography. *arXiv:1808.05860*. [Mathematical foundations for computational redistricting.]

Stephanopoulos, N. O., & McGhee, E. M. (2015). Partisan gerrymandering and the efficiency gap. *University of Chicago Law Review*, 82(2), 831-900. [Legal framework for the efficiency gap as a judicially manageable standard.]

## Deliberative Democracy

Fishkin, J. S. (2009). *When the People Speak: Deliberative Democracy and Public Consultation*. Oxford University Press. [The definitive account of Deliberative Polling methodology and results.]

Fishkin, J. S. (2018). *Democracy When the People Are Thinking: Revitalizing Our Politics Through Public Deliberation*. Oxford University Press. [Updated arguments and evidence for deliberative democracy.]

Dryzek, J. S. (2000). *Deliberative Democracy and Beyond: Liberals, Critics, Contestations*. Oxford University Press. [Theoretical foundations of deliberative democracy.]

Fishkin, J. S., & Luskin, R. C. (2005). Experimenting with a democratic ideal: Deliberative polling and public opinion. *Acta Politica*, 40(3), 284-298. [Empirical results from deliberative polls.]

## Electoral Systems and Reform

Lijphart, A. (1999). *Patterns of Democracy: Government Forms and Performance in Thirty-Six Countries*. Yale University Press. [Comparative analysis of majoritarian vs. consensus democracies.]

Richie, R. (2004). Instant runoff voting: What Mexico (and others) could learn. *Election Law Journal*, 3(3), 501-512. [Practical arguments for ranked-choice voting.]

FairVote. (2022). *Ranked Choice Voting in Alaska: 2022 Election Results and Analysis*. [Data source for the Alaska RCV case study in Chapter 13.]

## Media and Political Communication

Sunstein, C. R. (2017). *#Republic: Divided Democracy in the Age of Social Media*. Princeton University Press. [Echo chambers and filter bubbles as threats to democratic discourse.]

Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1981). The framing of decisions and the psychology of choice. *Science*, 211(4481), 453-458. [The foundational framing effect — connected to the 8.9sigma result via *Geometric Communication*.]

Bail, C. A., et al. (2018). Exposure to opposing views on social media can increase political polarization. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 115(37), 9216-9221. [Evidence that algorithmic exposure to opposing views does not reduce polarization and may increase it.]

Bond, A. H. (2026i). *Geometric Education*. [Book 9: Learning manifold, assessment as contraction, pedagogical trajectories.]

Bond, A. H. (2026j). *Geometric Politics: Representation, Polarization, and the Topology of Democratic Choice*. [Book 10 — this volume.]

do Carmo, M. P. (1992). *Riemannian Geometry*. Birkhauser. [Standard reference for Riemannian manifolds, geodesics, and curvature.]

Edelsbrunner, H., & Harer, J. (2010). *Computational Topology: An Introduction*. American Mathematical Society. [Persistent homology and topological data analysis.]

Nash, J. (1950). Equilibrium points in n-person games. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 36(1), 48-49. [The Nash equilibrium — the scalar special case of the Bond Geodesic Equilibrium.]

Noether, E. (1918). Invariante Variationsprobleme. *Nachrichten von der Gesellschaft der Wissenschaften zu Gottingen, Mathematisch-Physikalische Klasse*, 235-257. [Symmetry implies conservation — the mathematical foundation for the series' gauge-invariance program.]

Duverger, M. (1954). *Political Parties: Their Organization and Activity in the Modern State*. Wiley. [Duverger's Law: plurality voting produces two-party systems.]

Hohfeld, W. N. (1913). Some fundamental legal conceptions as applied in judicial reasoning. *Yale Law Journal*, 23(1), 16-59. [The eight jural relations whose $D_4$ symmetry appears throughout the series.]
